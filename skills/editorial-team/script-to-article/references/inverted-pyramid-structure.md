# Inverted Pyramid Structure

Reference guide for structuring news articles using the inverted pyramid approach. Use this when restructuring spoken content into written reading order (Workflow 1, Step 4).

## Three-Layer Structure

**Layer 1 — The Lede (first 1-2 paragraphs):** Most newsworthy information. Who did what, when, where, why it matters. If readers only read this, they have the news.

**Layer 2 — Essential Context (next 3-5 paragraphs):** Key details for understanding significance. Background, immediate context, most important quotes, primary evidence. If readers stop here, they understand the story completely.

**Layer 3 — Additional Details (remaining paragraphs):** Secondary information, additional quotes, fuller background, related details. Enriches understanding but not essential to grasp main news.

**Why it works:** Readers scan (scanners still get essentials), editors cut from bottom (least important goes last), digital readers leave early (they still got the news).

## Crafting Ledes

### The 5Ws Framework

Include the most newsworthy Who, What, When, Where, Why (or How) — not necessarily all five, but the most important ones.

**Example:** "City Council voted 5-4 Tuesday to approve a controversial $120 million development project downtown, ending six months of heated public debate over gentrification concerns."

### The So-What Test

Every lede must answer: why should readers care? If you cannot articulate significance, you need a different lede.

Without so-what: "City Council approved rent control Tuesday."

With so-what: "City Council approved rent control Tuesday, a policy that will affect 45,000 rental units and could cap annual rent increases at 5%."

### Common Lede Mistakes

**Cramming everything into one sentence:** Break complex information into 2 sentences. 57 words of mush becomes two clear sentences with the same information.

Bad: "Mayor Jane Smith, who was elected last November and campaigned on affordable housing, announced Tuesday at City Hall during a press conference attended by dozens of reporters and community members that the city would implement a new rent control policy affecting buildings with more than four units that has been debated for six months and opposed by landlord groups."

Better: "The city will implement rent control on buildings with more than four units, Mayor Jane Smith announced Tuesday. The policy, debated for six months and strongly opposed by landlord groups, represents a major shift in city housing strategy."

**Burying the lede:** Starting with background instead of news. Get to the point first, then provide context.

**Being too cute or clever:** This is news, not features. Give readers the news first.

## Quote Selection and Attribution

### When to Quote vs Paraphrase

**Quote when:**
- Words are colorful, distinctive, or revealing
- Official statements requiring exact language
- Emotional responses that paraphrase would flatten
- Expert analysis stated precisely

**Paraphrase when:**
- Information is routine
- Quote is verbose or unclear
- You can state it more concisely
- Multiple sources say the same thing

### Attribution Rules

- **"Said" is best.** Do not use "claimed," "admitted," "revealed," "stated" — these imply judgment
- **Attribution placement:** Mid-sentence or end, not beginning. "Smith said" mid-quote is less intrusive than "According to Smith" at the start
- **Do not over-attribute:** Once speaker is established, attribution is not needed every sentence if clear from context
- **Direct vs indirect:** Do not mix. Either fully direct (exact words in quotation marks) or fully indirect (paraphrased, no quotation marks)

## AP Style Quick Reference

**Titles:** Capitalize formal titles before names, lowercase after. "President Joe Biden" but "Joe Biden, president of the United States."

**Numbers:** Spell out one through nine, numerals for 10+. Exceptions (always numerals): ages, money, time, addresses, percentages.

**Dates and time:** Month Day, Year (no commas in month-year). Use a.m./p.m. (lowercase with periods).

**Punctuation in quotes:** Periods and commas inside quotation marks. Semicolons and colons outside.

## Clarity Principles

- **Short sentences:** 20-25 words maximum. Break up long complex sentences
- **Active voice:** "Council approved the policy" not "The policy was approved by council"
- **Concrete language:** Specific nouns and verbs. "The mayor vetoed the bill" not "The mayor took action on the legislation"
- **Define terms:** Do not assume readers know jargon. Define technical terms on first use
- **One idea per sentence:** Three clauses explaining three things should be three sentences

## Revision Checklist

### Tightening

- **Eliminate redundancy:** "past history" -> "history", "future plans" -> "plans", "completely destroyed" -> "destroyed", "end result" -> "result"
- **Cut throat-clearing:** Delete sentences that do not add information
- **Strengthen verbs:** "made a decision to veto" -> "vetoed"
- **Delete qualifiers:** "somewhat," "very," "quite," "rather" usually weaken prose

### Read-Aloud Test

Read the story aloud. Stumble over a sentence? Readers will too. Run out of breath? Sentence is too long. Sounds awkward? It is — rewrite it.

### Pre-Filing Accuracy Check

- Verify spelling of all names (people, places, organizations)
- Confirm titles and affiliations
- Double-check numbers, dates, addresses
- Review quotes against notes or recordings
- Confirm attribution is accurate
- Check that the lede reflects the reporting

## Common Mistakes

- **Editorializing:** No "unfortunately" or "thankfully" in news stories. Let facts speak
- **Assuming knowledge:** Provide context; do not assume readers remember previous stories
- **Lazy attribution:** "Some people say" or "experts believe" — name sources or do not cite them
- **Burying key information:** Readers who leave after three paragraphs should still know the core story
